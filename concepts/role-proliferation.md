# Role Proliferation

The organizational anti-pattern where problems are "solved" by creating new roles rather than addressing root causes. The pattern:

> Problem [X] exists → discomfort with how to solve it → create role [X]-manager or [X]-coordinator → find a person → the original problem is never solved → the role *maintains* the problem because solving it would eliminate the role's reason for existence.

Result: ownership evaporates, purpose disappears, work fragments further, and coordination overhead grows.

## The Taylorist root

Frederick Taylor's scientific management (early 1900s) made work fragmentation into a principle: break every task into the smallest possible unit, assign a specialist, optimize each unit independently. Henry Ford's assembly line was its physical expression.

Taylor's legacy in modern organizations:
- Extreme role specialization ("everyone does their piece")
- Separation of thinking from doing (managers plan, workers execute)
- Coordination as a management task, not a team responsibility
- Performance measured by individual task completion, not value delivered

The paradox: Taylor's method works perfectly in *Complicated* domains (assembly lines, repeatable processes). It fails catastrophically in *Complex* domains — which is where most modern knowledge work lives.

## The S3 view on roles

In Sociocracy 3.0, a role is a **solution pattern**:
- Created to solve a specific problem within a specific domain
- Held for a **limited term**, assigned by consent
- Dissolved when the problem is solved or the domain changes
- Separate from personal identity — you *keep* a role, you are not *the* role

This requires **role fluidity maturity**: the organizational and personal capacity to create, hand over, and dissolve roles without existential threat.

In most organizations, this maturity is absent. People identify *as* their role ("I am a Project Manager"). Role dissolution feels like identity loss. This is compounded when organizational culture rewards role tenure over problem-solving.

## Connection to our work

**Section 3 (Work Deconstruction)** — for each role involved in a step, ask: *what problem was this role created to solve, and is that problem solved or maintained?*

**Section 5 (Brutal Step Analysis)** — "Is this step a symptom of another problem?" often traces back to a role that was created to manage a problem that should have been eliminated.

**Section 8 (Greenfield)** — in AI-native design, roles should be solution patterns with clear scope, limited term, and dissolution criteria. Not identities.

**Section 14 (Expected Emergence)** — role fluidity is both a target and a threat: the transformation will create and dissolve roles faster than most org cultures can absorb.
