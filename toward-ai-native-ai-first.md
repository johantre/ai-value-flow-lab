# Toward AI-Native: A Credible Path

---

## The core tension

"AI-first" sounds like a blank slate — throw everything away and redesign from scratch.
That is intellectually honest. It is also organizationally impossible.

No real organization can ignore its people, its customers, its institutional knowledge, its regulatory constraints, or its running systems. Tabula rasa does not sell. It does not survive contact with reality.

But the opposite — adding AI tools to existing processes incrementally — is equally insufficient. It makes broken processes faster. It optimizes the wrong things. It produces "AI-enhanced" organizations that are still fundamentally organized around human limitations.

**The answer is not in the middle.** The answer is a different movement entirely:

> Design the AI-native end state as if starting from scratch.
> Build the path toward it from where you actually are.
> Make every step on that path a real, working state — not just a phase.

This is not a compromise. It is a discipline.

## Domain Awareness: The Cynefin Distinction

*(Dave Snowden, Cynefin framework — Cognitive Edge)*

Not all problems are the same kind of problem. Dave Snowden's Cynefin framework identifies five distinct domains, each requiring a fundamentally different way of acting:

| Domain | Cause & Effect | Right approach | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Clear** | Obvious, immediate | Sense → Categorize → Respond | Processing an invoice |
| **Complicated** | Requires expertise | Sense → Analyze → Respond | Engineering a bridge |
| **Complex** | Only visible in retrospect | Probe → Sense → Respond | Organizational transformation |
| **Chaotic** | No cause & effect | Act → Sense → Respond | Crisis management |
| **Disorder** | Unknown | First: figure out which domain you're in | — |

AI-first organizational transformation sits in the **Complex** domain.

Why? Because:
- The organization changes as you change it — people react, adapt, resist, improvise
- AI capabilities evolve during the transformation — what is Genesis today becomes Product tomorrow
- What works in one team fails in another, for reasons you cannot fully predict
- Cause and effect are only visible in retrospect
- There are no best practices — only emergent practices

**The implication is severe:**

In Complicated: you can plan, follow steps, apply expertise, predict outcomes.
In Complex: you cannot. Any plan that claims to predict the outcome of an organizational transformation is either misreading the domain — or lying.

**But not everything is Complex:**

| Activity | Domain | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Mapping current value streams | Complicated | Analyzable with expertise |
| Assessing AI capabilities per step | Complicated | Expert judgment, researchable |
| Choosing which AI tool to buy | Complicated | Analyzable, comparable |
| The organizational transformation itself | **Complex** | Emergent, non-linear, unpredictable |
| Cultural change | **Complex** | Human systems are inherently complex |
| Leadership behavior under pressure | **Complex** | Context-dependent, non-linear |

**This splits our work into two fundamentally different activities:**

**Part I — Landscape Mapping (Complicated)**
Use expertise, analysis, and structured frameworks to understand the current state and design the target direction. Templates and structured analysis are appropriate here.

**Part II — Probe Design (Complex)**
Design safe-to-fail experiments. Observe what emerges. Amplify what works. Dampen what doesn't. Never call it a plan — call it a set of hypotheses.

This distinction runs through everything that follows.

## The AI Maturity Spectrum

Not every value stream needs to reach the same level. Knowing where you are — and where you realistically need to go — is the first honest act.

| Level | Name            | What it looks like                                                      |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| 0     | AI-unaware      | Processes designed entirely for human execution. No AI involved.        |
| 1     | AI-assisted     | Humans use AI tools occasionally. Process design unchanged.             |
| 2     | AI-augmented    | AI embedded in workflows. Humans still make all decisions.              |
| 3     | AI-native       | Processes redesigned around AI capabilities. Humans define boundaries.  |
| 4     | AI-driven       | AI makes most operational decisions. Humans set strategy and exceptions.|

Most organizations today sit at Level 0–1 and call it Level 2.
Most "AI transformations" target Level 2 and call it AI-first.

**The question is not: how do we get everything to Level 4?**
The question is: **which value streams need to reach Level 3–4 to change the competitive game, and which can stay at Level 2?**

## The Capability Evolution Lens

*(Derived from Wardley Mapping — Simon Wardley)*

Every capability — technology, process, AI tool — evolves along a predictable curve, whether you want it to or not:

```
Genesis → Custom Built → Product → Commodity
```

**Genesis** — New, experimental, poorly understood. High risk, high potential for differentiation. Nobody has industrialized this yet.

**Custom Built** — Understood enough to build, but each organization does it their own way. Expensive, bespoke, still differentiating.

**Product** — Available off the shelf. You buy it, not build it. Still adds value, but no longer differentiates.

**Commodity** — Ubiquitous, standardized, utility. Like electricity. You plug it in and don't think about it.

**Applied to AI capabilities:**

| Capability | 2020 | 2023 | 2025 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Training a language model | Genesis | Custom Built | Product |
| Using a language model via API | Custom Built | Product | Commodity |
| AI image recognition | Custom Built | Product | Commodity |
| Autonomous AI agents | Genesis | Genesis | Custom Built |
| Domain-specific reasoning | Genesis | Custom Built | Custom Built |

**The strategic implication:**

- If a capability is **Commodity**: buy it, don't build it. Focus your energy elsewhere.
- If a capability is **Product**: evaluate buy vs. build based on fit and cost.
- If a capability is **Custom Built**: building gives differentiation — but only for as long as it stays there.
- If a capability is **Genesis**: high risk, high potential. This is where competitive advantage is forged — before everyone else can buy the same solution.

**The mistake most organizations make:**
- Treating **Commodity** as Genesis — building internally what they could just buy. Wasted investment.
- Treating **Genesis** as Commodity — buying a standard solution for something that could have been their differentiator. Strategic loss.

**The question this forces:**

> Which AI capabilities in your industry are still Genesis or Custom Built — and represent a window of differentiation that will close within 2–3 years?

That window is where anchor transformations should focus.

## Why current state still matters

The current state is not the enemy. It contains:

- Real institutional knowledge about customers, edge cases, and failure modes
- Existing data — imperfect, but real
- People who understand the domain, even if the process around them is broken
- Relationships, trust, and organizational memory

An AI-native design that ignores these will fail at implementation.
The current state is the **launchpad**, not the problem.

The problem is treating the current state as the **ceiling**.

## The method: Design backwards, build forwards

### Step 1: Design the AI-native end state (unconstrained)

Before looking at what exists, answer this:

*If you were building this value stream today, with AI as a native capability, from scratch — what would it look like?*

- What steps would not exist?
- What would be continuous instead of periodic?
- What would be automated instead of human-executed?
- What feedback loops would be closed that are currently open?
- What decisions currently made on intuition would be data-driven?
- What would the customer experience look like if latency, cost, and human error were near zero?

This is your **target architecture**. It does not need to be achievable tomorrow. It needs to be honest.

### Step 2: Map the current state (honestly)

Now look at what actually exists. Not to validate it — to understand the delta.

- For each value stream: what level (0–4) is it at today?
- Where is the real value being created, and where is it being consumed by coordination, control, and waste?
- What data exists, and what is its quality?
- Where do humans compensate for broken processes or missing information?

### Step 3: Find the delta

Lay the target architecture next to the current state.

- Where is the gap largest?
- Where does closing the gap require new data, new skills, or new systems?
- Where does closing the gap mostly require permission — not new technology?

### Step 4: Identify anchor transformations

Not all gaps are equal. Some transformations, if done first, pull others along.
These are your **anchor transformations** — the 1–3 places where AI-native redesign changes the fundamental economics or possibilities of the organization.

Criteria for an anchor transformation:
- It changes the unit economics significantly (cost, speed, or quality)
- It generates data that enables further AI capabilities
- It removes a bottleneck that currently constrains the whole value stream
- The AI capability it relies on is not yet Commodity — giving you a window of differentiation before competitors can simply buy the same solution
- OR: it is visible enough to build organizational credibility for what follows

### Step 5: Design safe-to-fail probes — not a transition plan

*(Complex domain: Probe → Sense → Respond)*

You cannot plan an organizational transformation. You can design experiments that let you observe what emerges — and respond accordingly.

These are **safe-to-fail probes**: small, reversible experiments that move toward the target attractor without betting the organization on a single path.

Each probe must be:
- **Safe to fail** — if it fails, the organization recovers without serious damage
- **Parallel, not sequential** — run multiple probes simultaneously; don't wait for one to finish
- **Observable** — you can tell within a defined timeframe whether it's working
- **Directional** — it moves toward the target attractor, not away from it

For each probe, define:
- **Hypothesis:** "We believe that if we [do X], we will observe [Y], which will tell us [Z]"
- **Safe-to-fail boundary:** maximum cost or impact if this fails?
- **Sensing signal:** what do we watch? What does "working" look like in practice?
- **Amplifier:** if it works — how do we do more of it, faster?
- **Dampener:** if it fails — how do we reduce or stop it quickly?

### Step 6: Design the sensing cadence

In Complex, the sensing cadence is more important than the plan. You cannot manage what you cannot observe — and in emergence, you need to observe from multiple vantage points.

Define:
- How often do you check in on what's emerging? (weekly? per sprint? monthly?)
- Who observes — and from what vantage points? (leadership, frontline, customers, data)
- What signals indicate something is working?
- What signals indicate something is failing or creating unexpected side effects?
- What is the decision trigger: when do you amplify, dampen, or pivot?

### Step 7: Define what to preserve

Some things in the current state are inputs to the new model, not obstacles to it:
- Domain expertise that AI cannot replicate (yet)
- Customer relationships built on human trust
- Regulatory and compliance knowledge embedded in current roles
- Data and institutional memory that will train and inform AI systems
- The people who know where the bodies are buried — and why certain decisions were made

Identify these explicitly. Losing them in the transformation is a risk most AI-first redesigns underestimate.

## What the evidence actually shows about people and change

Before designing any transformation narrative, start from what is empirically true about humans — not what the change management industry assumes.

### The resistance myth

The dominant assumption: *people resist change, therefore resistance must be managed.*

This is wrong in a specific way. Homo sapiens is the most adaptive large-bodied species on this planet. The species has survived ice ages, colonized every continent, and reorganized its entire way of living repeatedly across millennia. "People resist change" is not a biological observation. It is a description of what people do in a particular *context* — one that feels threatening, opaque, or controlled.

**The fence analogy**: two dogs separated by a rolling fence. Fence closed: aggression, territorial behavior, barking. Fence opened: "hey buurman" — curiosity, sniffing, connection. Same dogs. Same instincts. Different context, completely different behavior.

The same pattern appears in organizations. The "resistant employee" in a siloed, control-heavy, low-trust environment is behaving rationally *for that environment*. Remove the fence — change the context — and you get fundamentally different behavior from the same people, without a single change management intervention.

**The implication**: we do not need to manage resistance. We need to manage the *context*. AI is part of that context — and which context it creates is a design choice, not a given.

### What people actually report loving about work

Evidence from hundreds of retrospectives, pre-COVID, sticky notes on walls:

- *"Wat hebben wij goe samengewerkt"* — among the most frequent positive observations teams write about a two-week sprint
- *"Ik heb iets bijgeleerd, ik vond dat fantastisch"* — learning together as a consistent source of genuine joy

These are not exceptional moments. They are the ground state of what people report when given a context that enables it. The theoretical confirmation:

- **Sapiens** (Harari): large-scale cooperation through shared narrative is the defining capability of homo sapiens — not individual intelligence, not physical strength
- **De meeste mensen deugen** (Bregman): empirical historical evidence that people default to mutual aid and cooperation in crisis — the opposite of "veneer theory." Systems designed around distrust produce the behavior they fear (nocebo effect)
- **Drive** (Pink): intrinsic motivation — autonomy, mastery, purpose — is not an exception. It is the default when context allows it

### The medium-distance-moral standards warning

Information networks that increase social distance lower moral standards. This is not a moral failing — it is neurological. The brain regulates social behavior partly based on perceived proximity and consequence. Remove proximity, and the regulatory signal weakens.

Email unleashed workplace rudeness that face-to-face meetings contained. Twitter and Instagram enable behavior toward strangers that nobody would exhibit in person. The pattern is consistent: **greater mediation = greater distance = lower threshold for harmful behavior.**

**This is the hidden risk of AI-mediated organizations.** Poorly designed AI systems replicate this pattern at scale:
- Automated decisions with no human author → people have no one to be accountable to
- AI performance monitoring → surveillance without dialogue → Bregman's nocebo in action
- Async AI tools replacing synchronous conversation → relationship signals disappear
- AI-mediated rejection or feedback → the human on the receiving end becomes invisible

**Nexus** (Harari) frames this as the error-correction problem: information networks without error-correction become dangerous. The printing press enabled the Reformation and witch trials simultaneously. Social media enabled both coordination and radicalization. AI organizations need built-in error-correction — which is exactly what the sensing cadence (Section 12) and amplifiers/dampeners (Section 13) are designed to provide.

**Well-designed AI systems invert the distance problem:**
- AI absorbs coordination friction → people can have more real conversations, not fewer
- AI handles transactional interactions → humans handle relational ones
- AI surfaces signals early → humans respond with context and judgment before damage compounds
- AI takes the isolated, fragmented tasks → more room for the collaborative work people already love

The design question is never "how much AI?" It is: **what kind of social distance does this design create — and is that the right distance for this interaction?**

## The narrative problem

Even a technically sound AI-native path will fail if it cannot be told as a believable story inside the organization.

The story that works is not:
> "We are replacing how we work."

The story that works is not even:
> "We are freeing people from the work that should never have been human work."

*(True — but abstract. People need to feel it, not just understand it.)*

The story that actually lands is:
> "We are changing the context in which you work — to give you more of what you already love: real collaboration, real learning, work that matters — and less of what drains you: fragmented tasks, coordination overhead, doing the same thing for the tenth time."

This lands because it is *recognizable*. People know exactly what they love about work. They wrote it on sticky notes. The transformation narrative needs to connect directly to that felt experience — not to abstract organizational efficiency.

**The test for any AI-native design**: does it give people more of what they write on retro sticky notes? If not, reconsider the design — not the communication strategy.

AI should absorb: coordination overhead, repetitive judgment calls, information retrieval, pattern recognition at scale, waiting, isolated execution.

Humans should retain: contextual judgment in novel situations, customer relationships that require trust, ethical decisions, creative problem-solving, learning together, collective sense-making.

If your AI-native design does not reflect this distinction — if it takes away collaboration and learning rather than restoring them — it is not credible. And it should not be sold as progress.

### The widesizing frame

The dominant fear narrative — "AI destroys jobs" — is structurally incomplete. A more accurate frame (Appelo, 2026):

> AI lowers the minimum viable size of a business. Work does not disappear — it reorganizes into smaller units.

Whether economic activity is distributed across one 100,000-person company, 1,000 firms of 100 employees, or 100,000 independent operators: the total remains roughly constant. Only the structure changes. Corporate layoffs are visible; millions of micro-businesses launching in spare bedrooms are not. This is visibility bias, not economic collapse.

**The strategic question this forces** — for every organization doing this assessment:

> Which activities do we want to keep inside the organization, and which do we let the market do? And for those we keep: are we designing them to be worth keeping?

This reframes the AI-first transformation from a cost-cutting exercise into a deliberate choice about organizational identity. What you internalize defines what you are. What you outsource or automate defines what you are *not willing to be*.

**The bottleneck has shifted.** Previously, labor was scarce — the constraint was in human capacity and time. Now, good ideas, creativity, and adaptability are scarce — AI handles the rest. This has a direct implication for AI-first design: organizations that use AI to become more *efficient* are optimizing for the wrong game. Efficiency is becoming a commodity. The new competitive factors are speed of learning, creative capacity, and organizational adaptability. An AI-first transformation that produces a leaner headcount but a less creative, less adaptive organization has made things worse, not better.

**The honest risk**: not everyone can widessize. The salary bundle that corporate employment provides — income, benefits, training, legal protection, status, economic buffer — is real. Widesizing trades this for autonomy but introduces volatility. Designing AI-first transformations that eliminate roles without acknowledging this trade-off is neither honest nor sustainable. This connects directly to the viability check in the assessment template: talent pipeline, maintainability, organizational identity.

## What this means for the assessment template

The existing Value Stream Assessment Template is a strong diagnostic tool. It maps current state, identifies waste, and surfaces AI impact per step.

But used alone, it produces **AI-enhanced** outcomes, not AI-native ones.

To use it toward an AI-native outcome, run it in two passes:

**Pass 1 — Greenfield (unconstrained):**
Fill in Sections 8 and 9 first, before touching Sections 2–7.
Design the AI-native target architecture without looking at what exists.
This is your destination.

**Pass 2 — Current state (grounded):**
Now fill in Sections 2–7.
This is your launchpad.

**Then:**
Use the delta between Pass 1 and Pass 2 to identify anchor transformations and sequence the transition path.

The template then becomes a tool for designing a credible journey — not just a diagnostic of the present.
