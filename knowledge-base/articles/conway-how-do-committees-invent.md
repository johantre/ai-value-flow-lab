## Core idea

Any organization that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of that organization's communication structure. This is Conway's Law — the homomorphism between organizational communication and system architecture.

## What Conway actually said (three things often missed)

**1. The Law itself**
> "Organizations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations."

Not a metaphor — a mathematical claim. Each subsystem maps to a design group, and the interfaces between subsystems mirror the negotiated interfaces between those groups.

**2. Architecture is never complete — it is always in flux**
> "It is misleading and incorrect to speak of *the* design for a specific job, unless this is understood in the context of space, time, knowledge, and technology."
> "There is a *family* of system designs which will meet that requirement."

Architecture is not a stable artifact. It is a snapshot of the best understanding at a given moment. Anchoring teams to current architecture artifacts freezes teams to yesterday's design decisions.

**3. Teams should be flexible relative to architecture — not the other way around**
> "A design effort should be organized according to the need for communication."
> "Because the design which occurs first is almost never the best possible, the prevailing system concept may need to change. Therefore, flexibility of organization is important to effective design."

Conway's recommendation is the *opposite* of "build your org around your architecture." Build your org around your *communication needs* — which change as understanding matures.

## The two popular interpretations

**Conway's Law** (descriptive): your system will reflect your org structure, whether you want it to or not.

**The Inverse Conway Maneuver** (prescriptive, from *Team Topologies* — Skelton & Pais): deliberately design teams and communication structures to produce the system architecture you *want*, not the one you have. Use Conway's Law intentionally.

**Conway's own nuance**: the danger isn't designing teams around architecture — it's designing teams around *current* architecture artifacts, which are incomplete and will change. Design teams around the desired communication structure. Architecture follows.
