# Cooperation as Default

Humans are not fundamentally resistant to change. They are fundamentally *adaptive* — the most adaptive large-bodied species on this planet, which is precisely why homo sapiens has occupied and survived on every continent for tens of thousands of years. What humans respond to is **context**. Change the context, and behavior changes — without management, coaching, or persuasion.

## The resistance myth

The dominant change management narrative assumes:
> People resist change → resistance must be managed → communication, training, incentives are the levers.

This is backwards. People don't resist change in the abstract. They respond to specific context changes in ways that can feel like resistance — but are actually rational adaptations to the environment they perceive.

**The fence analogy**: two dogs, separated by a rolling fence. With the fence closed: aggression, territorial behavior, barking. Fence opens: "hey buurman" — sniffing, exploration, connection. Same dogs. Same instincts. **Different context = different behavior.**

Organizational change works the same way. The "resistant employee" in a siloed, control-heavy, low-trust environment is behaving rationally for that environment. Change the environment — remove the fence — and you get different behavior from the same people.

## What the evidence shows

Field evidence from retrospectives (hundreds of teams, pre-COVID sticky notes):
- **"What hebben wij goe samengewerkt"** — consistently among the most common positive observations
- **"Ik heb iets bijgeleerd, ik vond dat fantastisch"** — learning together as a source of genuine joy

These are not exceptional moments. They are the *ground state* of what humans report when given a context that enables it.

Theoretical confirmation:
- **Sapiens** (Harari): humans cooperate at civilizational scale through shared narratives. Cooperation, not competition, is the superpower.
- **De meeste mensen deugen** (Bregman): empirical historical evidence that humans default to cooperation and mutual aid in crisis — the opposite of what "veneer theory" predicts. Systems designed around distrust *create* the behavior they fear (nocebo effect).
- **Nexus** (Harari): information networks mediate human cooperation. The design of the network shapes what cooperation is possible.

## AI as context

AI is not a tool people use. **AI is part of the context people work in.** This distinction matters enormously for transformation design.

If AI-first design treats people as users of a tool, the design question is: "How do we get adoption?" (back to resistance management).

If AI-first design treats AI as context, the design question is: "What context are we creating — and does it enable or inhibit what humans naturally do well?"

**The right question is always about context, not about change.**

## The medium-distance-moral standards warning

Mediated communication increases social distance. Greater social distance lowers moral standards — people do things in email, Twitter, or Instagram DMs that they would never do face-to-face. This is not a moral failing; it is a neurological reality. The brain regulates behavior partly based on perceived proximity and social consequence.

**Poorly designed AI systems replicate this pattern:**
- AI-mediated performance reviews with no human author → people game the system without guilt
- Automated rejection systems → candidates feel invisible, organizations become callous
- Async AI tools that replace synchronous conversation → relationship signals disappear, conflict escalates
- Always-on AI monitoring → people feel surveilled, not trusted → Bregman's nocebo effect in action

**Well-designed AI systems invert this:**
- AI reduces coordination friction → people can have more real conversations
- AI handles the transactional → humans handle the relational
- AI surfaces signals → humans respond with context and judgment
- AI takes over the lonely, isolated tasks → more room for the collaborative work people love

The design question is not "how much AI?" but "what kind of social distance does this design create — and is that the right distance for this interaction?"

## Connection to our work

**The narrative layer** of [[../../toward-ai-native-ai-first]]: the transformation story is not "we are changing how you work." It is "we are changing the context in which you work — to give you more of what you already love (collaboration, learning, mastery) and less of what you don't (fragmented tasks, isolated execution, coordination overhead)."

**Section 0 (Strategic Orientation)**: before mapping processes, ask what people in this organization already love about their work. The transformation should amplify that.

**Section 9 (Design Principles)**: the design question is "what context does this AI system create?" — not "how do we drive adoption?"

**Section 11 (Safe-to-Fail Probes)**: design probes that test whether the new context enables natural cooperation — and whether AI is reducing or increasing social distance in the relevant interactions.

**Section 14 (Expected Emergence)**: watch for signs that AI is increasing rather than decreasing social distance — moral disengagement, reduced accountability, communication that becomes more transactional and less human.

**Warning**: a transformation that makes processes faster but increases social distance, reduces collaboration, and eliminates learning opportunities has not succeeded — it has replicated Taylor's mistake with better tools.
