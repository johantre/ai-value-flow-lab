## Core idea

Information networks (language, writing, printing, internet) shape civilizations. AI is a new kind of information network that can operate without human understanding or values. Questions of AI alignment and governance are civilization-level.

### Connection to our work

**AI as information network, not tool.** Nexus reframes AI not as a technology you deploy but as a new kind of information network that mediates how humans cooperate. An AI-first organization is not "an org that uses AI tools" — it is an org whose information flows are increasingly AI-mediated. This makes the design of those flows a governance question, not just a technical one.

**The medium-distance-moral standards pattern.** Information networks that increase social distance lower moral standards — not because people become worse, but because the neurological feedback that regulates social behavior (proximity, eye contact, consequence) is absent. Email unleashed workplace rudeness. Twitter unleashed public cruelty. Poorly designed AI systems will do the same inside organizations: automated decisions with no human author, monitoring without dialogue, async tools that replace conversation.

**Error-correction is the critical design variable.** Harari's core warning: information networks without error-correction mechanisms become dangerous at scale. The printing press enabled both the Reformation and witch trials. Social media enabled both coordination and radicalization. AI organizations need built-in error-correction: sensing cadences (Section 12), amplifiers and dampeners (Section 13), and human judgment at decision points that matter.

**The design question**: does this AI system bring people closer together (enable richer cooperation) or push them further apart (increase distance, reduce accountability, erode moral standards)? Both outcomes are possible. Which one depends entirely on how the network is designed.
